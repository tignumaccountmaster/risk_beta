Part One

What it is, and the why behind it.

01 - The Context

Personal Readiness Index: a human performance risk management instrument.

Every strategy, every transaction, every critical decision routes through one place. The cognitive and emotional state of the executive making it.

That state is not fixed. It builds, and it drains. Under load, AI-driven acceleration, complexity, and uncertainty, it degrades. And it shapes the quality of an outcome more than any framework, program, or advisory engagement layered on top of it. This is the layer almost everyone ignores. Leadership programs sharpen strategy. Business coaching sharpens thinking. Neither one measures the human state that decides whether any of it actually lands.

TIGNUM has spent over twenty years building the infrastructure to do exactly that. To diagnose this state, recalibrate it, prime it for the moments that matter, and prove the return.

The Personal Readiness Index is that instrument. It measures an executive’s readiness state against twenty years of correlated behavioral, physiological, and performance data.

It makes the invisible foundation of performance visible. And measurable.

The Index reads Personal Readiness across the three levels that decide how an executive shows up:

Brain and Physiology

How resilient you are, how balanced your nervous system is, and what your level of pain and tension is. The foundation underneath everything else.

Daily Challenges

How you handle mindset killers, how recharged and fueled you are, and how adaptable you stay across a demanding day.

Critical Moments

How mentally agile you are, how focused you are, and how well you multiply energy when the stakes are highest.

Each client receives a clear score per level, and one overall Personal Readiness score. A baseline you can act on, and return to.

02 - Personal Readiness and Risk Management

A risk profile, moved to where it actually lives.

Risk management means identifying and analyzing risk in an investment. And every project a client runs is an investment. It weighs the upside, the gains, against the downside, the leaks, measured against the project’s goals. At its core, risk is the probability of a loss.

We took that logic and moved it to where it actually lives. Inside the human running the project. An executive on a company’s value agenda carries a risk profile, the same way an asset does. The Beta is how we express it.

Two reference points make the Beta readable

The benchmark

Thousands of business professionals, scored across twenty years of data. This is the “market” every individual is measured against.

The threshold

The number 1. Above it, you multiply. Below it, you leak.

03 - The Personal Readiness Risk Beta

Knowing a score is useful. Knowing what it does to performance is the point.

This is where the Beta comes in. We borrow one idea from the world of risk. A single number that tells you whether something amplifies what it touches, or quietly erodes it.

For over twenty years, TIGNUM has gathered millions of data points before and after our support. Physiological markers, Sustainable Human Performance behaviors, behavioral styles, mindset killers, physical, cognitive, and emotional load, plus coaching requests and progress.

We normalize all of it onto a simple 0 to 2 scale. The Risk Beta represents the average variance of an individual’s or a team’s Personal Readiness, measured against the “market” of thousands of professionals in the business world.

The 0–2 scale

A score of 1 is neutral. Personal Readiness neither adds nor subtracts. You perform at expectation. A score equal to 1 yields a neutral impact on the performance equation. So a Beta of e.g. 0.85 is not a bad grade. It is a quantified leak. It says this executive is currently operating at a discount, both to their own capability, and to the benchmark. That gap is the opportunity.

Below 1 is Diminishing

A negative effect on KPIs, a negative effect on projects, and a rising burnout risk.

Where it started: the physical assessment

The instrument began with a full physical assessment, tracking objective markers such as aerobic fitness, blood pressure, body fat, and blood results, run alongside the performance-habits assessment that became the Readiness Index. We examined whether the two moved together, and found they correlated.

Because the behavioural assessment tracked the physical data closely. And because physical testing is logistically heavy, time-consuming, and raises understandable data-privacy concerns, we moved to the assessment alone. It captures what matters in a fraction of the time.
