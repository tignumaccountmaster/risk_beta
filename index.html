Excursion — Business Case

Human Performance Beta as a risk management instrument.

Risk management means identifying and analysing risk in an investment — and every project a client runs is an investment. It evaluates both upsides (gains) and downsides (leaks) against project goals. At its core, risk is the probability of a loss, measured using statistical methods. One of those methods is called Beta.

A leader can have deep functional expertise and a well-defined role — but those combine into impact only through their Personal Readiness. The Readiness Risk Beta is how we measure that multiplier: below 1.0, it diminishes everything else the leader brings.

In finance

Beta measures volatility
relative to the market

β = 1 The market. The benchmark. Exactly average volatility. All investments are measured against this.
β > 1 More volatile than the market. Higher potential return — but higher risk of loss.
β < 1 Less volatile than the market. Lower risk — but also lower upside potential.

The two risk types: Systematic risk affects everyone — a pandemic, a financial crisis, a market shock. Unsystematic risk is specific to a company or team — poor leadership, misaligned culture, performance drain.

The TIGNUM inversion

Human Performance Beta
runs the other way

β = 1 The performance benchmark. The minimum standard for a team entering a critical project.
β > 1 Multiplying. The team generates more capacity than it consumes. This is where you want to be.
β < 1 Diminishing. The team is a performance liability. Lower beta = higher risk to the project.

The inversion is intentional and important. In finance, you avoid high beta if you want safety. In human performance, you pursue high beta. A CFO can hold both concepts simultaneously — they are measuring the same underlying thing (risk relative to a benchmark) from opposite directions.

Both risk types, measured
S
Systematic Risk

Readiness for what nobody sees coming

Systematic risk hits everyone simultaneously — a pandemic, a geopolitical shock, a market collapse. The question is not whether it will happen but whether the leadership team has the capacity to adapt when it does.

The TIGNUM instrument captures this through agility and adaptability behaviours — specifically the Daily Challenges and Critical Moments levels, which measure how leaders perform under unexpected constraint and pressure.

U
Unsystematic Risk

Readiness within the team and the project

Unsystematic risk is specific to a team or organisation — it can be reduced through deliberate action. This is where TIGNUM operates most directly.

The instrument captures this through energy multiplication, performance resilience, and self-observation behaviours — the dimensions that determine whether a team amplifies or drains collective capacity under project pressure.

The benchmark

The project readiness
standard: 62.5%

While β 1.0 anchors to the population median (42%), expert judgment places the minimum readiness for critical project work at 62.5% — a score that on the beta scale equates to approximately β 1.49. This is a separate aspirational benchmark, not a second threshold.

Under this standard, only 13.1% of leaders currently meet or exceed the benchmark on their first assessment. This may feel intentional — but it is not. It is a real statement about a real risk. The 62.5% threshold is where the instrument reliably predicts the performance capacity that high-stakes leadership genuinely requires.

01 — The Threshold

Below 1.0, leaders cost more than they produce.

β < 1.0
Diminishing

The leader is consuming more performance capacity than they generate. Decision quality degrades under pressure. The cost to the organisation is invisible — until it materialises in a deal, a decision, or a departure.

— THRESHOLD 1.0 —
β > 1.0
Multiplying

The leader generates more performance capacity than they consume. Greater mental agility, faster recalibration under pressure, sustained clarity. They become the energy source for everyone around them — elevating teams, decisions, and every room they walk into.

Pre-assessment anchor

The 1.0 threshold is the historical median of all 38,686 pre-assessments — the very first assessment each leader ever completes, before any TIGNUM intervention. That median corresponds to a raw score of 42% on the assessment scale. The beta conversion (score ÷ 2.5 × 0.9399) maps that 42% to exactly 1.0 — so the two numbers describe the same point, expressed on different scales. Crossing 1.0 means moving beyond the global pre-intervention baseline. Because the assessment instrument was redesigned in 2023 (expanding from 42 to 51 questions with revised wording), each instrument generation is normalised to its own pre-assessment median. This keeps β 1.0 meaningful within each era and prevents the instrument change from appearing as a performance shift. Betas are directly comparable within an instrument generation; across the 2023 boundary they should be read with this in mind.

When individual betas are averaged across a leadership team, the result is the team beta — a single number that reflects the collective readiness of the group. A team beta below 1.0 is a measurable statement that this team, at this moment, is operating below the performance median of all leaders TIGNUM has ever measured on their first day.

Where it started — the physical assessment

The instrument began with a full physical assessment — tracking objective markers such as aerobic fitness, blood pressure, body fat, and blood results — run alongside the performance-habits assessment that became the Readiness Index. We examined whether the two moved together, and found they correlated.

Because the behavioural assessment tracked the physical data closely — and because physical testing is logistically heavy, time-consuming, and raises understandable data-privacy concerns — we moved to the assessment alone. It captures what matters in a fraction of the time.

Part Three

How does it work.

04 — Methodology

Five steps from answer to Beta.

The calculation is deterministic, transparent, and reproducible across all three assessment generations spanning 2011 to today.

I
Pillar Score
avg(answers per pillar)

Mean of all answers mapped to each Level × Pillar combination. Double-mapped questions contribute to both buckets independently.

II
Level Score
(M + N + Mv + R) / 4

Average of four pillar scores — Mindset, Nutrition, Movement, Recovery — for each level. All four carry equal weight within a level.

III
Overall Score
(1×BP + 2×DC + 3×CM) / 6

Weighted average. Critical Moments carries 3× weight. Divisor 6 is the sum of weights (1+2+3), keeping the result on the 1–5 scale.

IV
Raw Beta
Score / 2.5

Maps the 1–5 score onto a 0–2 beta axis. Score 1.0 → β 0.40. Score 2.5 → β 1.00. Score 5.0 → β 2.00.

V
Normalised Beta
Raw Beta × 0.9399

Anchored to the median of 38,686 pre-assessments (41.5% → Raw β 1.064 → × 0.9399 = 1.000). By definition, the median first-assessment beta equals 1.0. The threshold is derived from the data, not assumed.

Brain & Physiology
Foundational readiness — weight 1×

Sleep, nutrition, movement, recovery habits. The biological substrate everything else runs on. Necessary but not sufficient — a leader can sleep well and still collapse under negotiation pressure.

Daily Challenges
Operational readiness — weight 2×

Performance under continuous load. How the leader shows up across the unrelenting sequence of decisions, meetings, and transitions that define the executive work day.

Critical Moments
Peak performance capacity — weight 3×

Readiness for high-stakes negotiations, board presentations, and decisions where the outcome disproportionately determines enterprise value. The moments that separate ordinary from extraordinary.
